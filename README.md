# TropCool: Transformer World Model for Tropical Data-Center Cooling

## What I built

**TropCool** is a **transformer-based world model** that forecasts next-hour data-center (DC) cooling outcomes from a **24-hour context** of weather, plant configuration, operating setpoints, and IT load. The model is trained on **synthetic trajectories** generated by coupling a **physics-style DC cooling simulator** to **real hourly weather archives**ÔÇöapproximately **twenty years** of Open-Meteo history (2004ÔÇô2024) for **six Southeast Asian cities**: Cyberjaya, Singapore, Jakarta, Bangkok, Johor Bahru, and Ho Chi Minh City. Each timestep carries tropical-relevant drivers (dry-bulb temperature, humidity, wet bulb, time-of-day, tariff, and facility parameters) and predicts **rack inlet temperature**, **cooling power**, **tower water use**, **PUE**, and **operating cost**.

The world model is **option-2 conditioned**: the input includes not only the past 24 hours but also **planned next-hour controls and IT/electricity context**, matching how operators commit setpoints before observing the outcome. This makes the mapping from actions to next-hour targets **learnable** even when controls and loads are not perfectly autocorrelated.

**Calibration to real DC data:** I **fine-tuned** the pretrained world model on **public Kaggle-style operational CSVs** (building-scale HVAC and cold-aisle telemetry), with **column mapping, unit conversion (e.g., ┬░FÔćĺ┬░C, RTÔćĺMW), and hourly alignment**. Targets for that stage were kept **consistent with the simulatorÔÇÖs label space** so normalization and heads remain compatible with the main checkpoint. Together, **synthetic diversity at scale** plus **real-data adaptation** is meant to reduce sim-to-real gap without abandoning the structured target definitions (PUE, cost, water) used in training.

**Downstream use:** the same forward model powers a **Gymnasium environment** (`WorldModelEnv`) for reinforcement learning: each step applies **(chiller setpoint, CRAH fan speed, tower fan speed)**, rolls the transformer one hour forward on **real weather for a chosen city**, and returns reward as **negative predicted cost**, with a large penalty if predicted **rack inlet temperature exceeds 27 ┬░C**. Stable-Baselines3 **SAC** trains against this environmentÔÇö**orders of magnitude faster per step** than stepping the full physics simulator.

**How to read ÔÇťY %ÔÇŁ once training completes:** pick a single evaluation contract (e.g., calendar year **2019**, **720 h**, same seed), compute **mean total predicted cost** over six cities for baseline vs SAC, then **Y = 100 ├Ś (1 Ôłĺ cost_SAC / cost_baseline)**. For **lower-variance** scores over multiple weather slices, use `compare_policies.py --mean-over-scenarios` (defaults: **years 2018ÔÇô2020**, **start_rows 0 / 2160 / 4320**, **seeds 42ÔÇô44**, Cartesian product Ôćĺ **27 scenarios** per city); the script prints **per-city mean ┬▒ std cost_RM** and **Y** from the mean of per-city means. For **maximum coverage** of the shared archive (baseline and other policies use the **same** scenario list), use **`--eval-wide-coverage`**: intersection of calendar years across all six CSVs, evenly spaced **start_row** per year capped by the **shortest** city-year window, tunable via **`--eval-coverage-starts-per-year`** and **`--eval-seeds`** (prefer a single seed if scenario count is large). The world model is the **surrogate** for cost hereÔÇöfinal operations should still be validated against meters and safety limits.
